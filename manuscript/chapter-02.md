# Episode 2

## Eleven Minutes
The egg waited until Liora had found one clean towel.
Not two.
Not several.
One.
It was a narrow, suspicious towel with a faded duck embroidered in the corner and the brittle texture of fabric that had spent twelve years being morally superior in a cupboard.
Liora held it in both hands.
The egg knocked again.
Knock.
Knock.
Crack.
“Oh,” Liora said.
The green shell split from top to bottom with the calm inevitability of a policy decision made by people not standing near it.
A thread of orange light leaked out.
The nest of old receipts began to smoke.
“No,” Liora said.
The egg ignored this, which put it in immediate agreement with most municipal departments.
Cress bleated sleepily from the moonwell room.
“Stay there,” Liora called.
Cress gave a second bleat.
It sounded like a question.
“No, I do not know what it is.”
The Ledger flipped a page.
[HATCHING EVENT IN PROGRESS]
Location: Front Hall
Species: Unknown
Thermal Output: Increasing
Recommended Equipment:
- Heat-safe basin
- Damp towel
- Mineral ash
- Calm voice
Unavailable Equipment:
- Heat-safe basin
- Mineral ash
Current Towel: legally insufficient

Liora looked at the towel.
The duck looked back.
“Legally insufficient,” she said. “That is hurtful.”
The crack widened.
Something inside the egg chirruped.
Then hiccuped.
Then set fire to a receipt.
Liora dropped the towel over it.
The towel hissed.
The duck vanished first.
“Right,” Liora said. “We are revising the towel plan.”
The egg rocked.
Once.
Twice.
Then a tiny orange foot burst through the shell and landed squarely in the nest of receipts.
The receipts flamed blue.
Not a large flame.
Not a dangerous flame.
Yet.
A bright, offended little flame that seemed to believe paper had insulted its family.
Liora snatched up the tortoise teapot from the desk and poured the last of her tea over the receipts.
Steam billowed.
The teapot made a ceramic noise of betrayal.
“I will apologize properly when fewer things are burning.”
The egg exploded.
Not violently.
Messily.
Shell pieces pattered across the desk, the floor, Liora’s skirt, and the Ledger, which snapped itself shut with the injured dignity of a book that had not consented to breakfast.
In the middle of the receipts sat the smallest toad Liora had ever seen.
It was round.
It was damp.
It was orange-gold, freckled with ember-red spots, and glowing gently from inside as though someone had misplaced a lantern in its stomach.
Its eyes were enormous.
Its mouth was wider than seemed administratively necessary.
A wisp of smoke curled from one nostril.
The toad looked at Liora.
Liora looked at the toad.
The toad hiccuped.
A spark jumped out and landed on the missing mitten.
The mitten caught fire.
“Absolutely not.”
Liora grabbed the mitten with the clean towel, dropped it into the empty teacup, and clapped a saucer over the top.
The saucer rattled.
The toad blinked.
Then it smiled.
That was unfair.
“Do not be charming at me,” Liora said. “You are currently a hazard.”
The system chimed.
[EMERGENCY INTAKE IDENTIFIED]
Species: Ember-Toad
Age: Newly Hatched
Condition: Overheated | Hungry | Unregulated Sparking
Risk: Moderate fire hazard. High appetite probability.
Core Need: Stable heat sink and supervised cooling
Secondary Need: Breakfast
Suggested urgency: immediate

“Of course breakfast is involved.”
The ember-toad chirped.
Its tongue flicked out.
It ate a receipt.
The paper vanished with a tiny puff of smoke.
The toad’s glow brightened.
Liora stared.
“No.”
It ate another receipt.
“Those are not food.”
It ate a third receipt.
“They are barely records, but that is not the same thing.”
The Ledger opened again.
A fresh line wrote itself across the page.
Receipts consumed: 3
Administrative loss: minor
Fire risk: less minor
The toad burped.
A ring of smoke floated upward and drifted into the chandelier.
The chandelier sneezed dust.
Liora rubbed her forehead.
She had been custodian of Briarwick Monster Nursery for less than one night, and already she had a mooncalf in recovery, an illegal ember-toad, one deceased towel, and a teapot that would require emotional repair.
Also no staff.
Staff seemed important.
Staff would be someone else discovering that the nearest appropriate heat-safe basin was unlikely to be beneath a reception desk full of receipts and mittens.
“Stay,” she told the toad.
The toad stared at her.
“Stay means remain in place without combusting anything.”
The toad ate a shell fragment.
“That was not an invitation to improvise.”
Liora picked up the wastepaper basket and placed it upside down over the toad.
The basket glowed.
Smoke escaped through the wicker.
The basket lifted two inches as the toad bounced beneath it.
Bounce.
Bounce.
Scorch.
Liora lifted the basket.
There was a perfect round burn mark on the floor.
The toad looked pleased with itself.
“Right. Not containment.”
It chirped.
“Do not agree with me. It encourages bad management.”
From the moonwell room came a soft clatter.
Cress appeared in the doorway.
He had the slow, uncertain gait of a creature who had been asleep, underfed, recently named, and still somehow determined to supervise. Moonlight clung to his chipped horn. His ears tilted forward.
He saw the toad.
The toad saw him.
For one whole breath, neither creature moved.
Then the toad’s glow pulsed brighter.
Cress sneezed silver dust.
The toad sneezed smoke.
The smoke ring passed through the silver dust and made a tiny floating star that fizzled above the desk.
Cress stared.
The toad looked deeply proud.
“No introductions until we have reduced the chance of architectural regret,” Liora said.
The word regret reminded the ceiling that it had been cracked for twelve years.
A small piece of plaster dropped into the umbrella stand.
The sword with the knitted cozy rattled.
Liora pointed at the ceiling.
“Not now.”
The ceiling, to its credit, stopped.
Mostly.
The system chimed again.
[CARE TASK: Thermal Stabilization]
Resident: Ember-Toad
Time to Thermal Spike: 9 minutes, 12 seconds
Required:
1. Relocate hatchling away from paper, wood, curtains, and emotionally important textiles.
2. Prepare heat-safe basin.
3. Create cooling ring with available materials.
4. Feed appropriate ember-compatible starter meal.
5. Maintain calm handling.

Available Resource:
Hearthglow: 4

Potential Hearthglow Uses:
- Temporary cooling ward
- Basin reinforcement
- Spark suppression

Liora stared at the list.
Then at the room.
The front hall contained paper, wood, curtains, textiles, a knitted sword cozy, dried ivy in a broken umbrella stand, and one leather Ledger that was probably emotionally important to itself.
“Excellent,” she said. “We live in kindling.”
The toad bounced.
A spark landed near the desk leg.
Cress made a worried sound.
“I saw it.”
Liora stamped the spark out with her shoe.
Her shoe smoked.
“Also excellent.”
She scooped up the toad.
This was a mistake.
The toad was warmer than a hot brick, slipperier than soap, and entirely convinced that hands existed to help it visit new objects with its mouth.
It wriggled up her sleeve.
Liora froze.
“No.”
The sleeve glowed.
“No, no, no.”
The toad’s head emerged near her elbow, delighted.
Liora held her arm away from her body with the concentration of a woman defusing a social situation at a council meeting.
“You are inside municipal cotton,” she said. “That cotton has suffered enough.”
The toad chirped.
It licked her cuff.
A small smoking hole appeared.
“Personal liability,” Liora muttered. “I am beginning to appreciate the phrase.”
Cress stepped forward.
His horn glowed.
Not much.
A thin silver thread of light drifted from the chipped crescent and touched the toad’s nose.
The toad stopped wriggling.
Its eyes crossed.
Then it sneezed into Liora’s sleeve.
The sneeze was warm.
The smoke was not ideal.
But the toad stilled.
Liora looked at Cress.
“Thank you.”
Cress blinked.
The system chimed softly.
[ASSISTED CARE OBSERVED]
Resident: Cress has reduced hatchling distress.
Cress Bond Trust: 19% → 20%
Note: Peer comfort detected.
Additional Note: This is emotionally efficient.

“Emotionally efficient,” Liora said. “Good. We are optimizing affection now.”
The toad relaxed against her wrist.
It made a low peeping sound.
Liora’s sleeve continued to smoke.
“Basin,” she said.
The kitchen was the least terrible option.
This was not the same as a good option.
She carried the toad at arm’s length through the hall while Cress followed behind on awkward hooves, looking like a worried moonbeam that had not been issued proper legs.
Bertram watched from beside the front door.
“Mm,” he said.
“Yes,” Liora said. “I know.”
“Mm.”
“No one asked you to look that unsurprised.”
“Mm.”
The kitchen welcomed them with dust, copper pans, a stove that had recently been complimented into service, and shelves full of jars whose labels had either faded, peeled, or committed perjury.
The toad lifted its head.
The stove glowed.
The stove, Liora was beginning to suspect, had opinions.
The toad chirped at it.
The stove gave a pleased little clank.
“No,” Liora told both of them.
The stove dimmed.
The toad’s lower lip trembled.
It was a very small lower lip.
That made it worse.
“We are not lighting the kitchen to improve morale.”
The toad’s lip trembled harder.
Cress nudged Liora’s hip.
“Oh, don’t you start.”
Cress gave her the look.
The mooncalf had known her less than a day and had already developed the look.
It was a serious operational concern.
“Fine. We are lighting one supervised flame.”
The stove preened so aggressively that all four brass knobs gleamed.
“One,” Liora said.
The stove produced six.
“Mathematics, please.”
Five flames vanished.
The remaining flame burned low and blue.
The toad sighed.
It sounded like a kettle being forgiven.
Liora set it in the largest mixing bowl she could find.
The bowl was ceramic.
It was not heat-safe.
She knew this because the bottom immediately made a cracking sound.
“Out.”
She lifted the toad.
The bowl split neatly in half.
The toad looked down.
Then at Liora.
Then at the broken bowl.
It peeped.
“I agree. Poor construction.”
The cupboards offered a copper cauldron, two dented pans, a pie dish shaped like a hedgehog, and a blackened iron washbasin under the sink.
The washbasin had old ward marks around its rim.
It also had a spider in it.
The waistcoated spider.
It looked up from the basin with eight eyes and the kind of offended posture that required no translation.
“I know,” Liora said. “I am back.”
The spider tapped one delicate foot.
“I need the basin.”
Tap.
“There is a fire baby.”
Tap.
“It is not my phrase. It is my situation.”
The spider considered the toad.
The toad considered the spider.
The toad opened its mouth.
“No eating staff.”
The spider froze.
The toad closed its mouth.
The spider lifted both front legs in a gesture that might have been outrage or invoice preparation.
Liora relocated the spider to a chipped saucer beside a jar of pearl barley.
It adjusted its waistcoat.
“Thank you for your understanding.”
The spider did not understand.
The spider endured.
That was close enough.
The washbasin was heavy, soot-streaked, and cold in the way old iron was cold when it had been waiting to be useful and had taken the waiting personally. Liora hauled it onto the floor near the stove.
It scraped the tiles.
Cress flinched.
The toad spark-hiccuped.
A blue flame jumped onto a dishcloth.
Liora slapped it with the insufficient towel.
The towel lost a corner.
“Still with us,” she told it.
The towel smoked bravely.
She poured clean water into the basin from the pump.
The water hissed against the iron.
Steam rose.
The toad leaned toward it.
“No diving.”
It leaned harder.
“Especially no diving.”
She needed ash.
Not ordinary ash, probably.
Mineral ash.
The task had specifically said mineral ash.
Briarwick’s kitchen shelves had many powders. They had powders in jars, powders in crocks, powders in paper twists, and one powder in a bottle labelled ABSOLUTELY NOT CINNAMON. Liora opened three and sneezed twice.
The first was flour.
The second was salt.
The third tried to hum.
She closed it.
The fourth jar read HEARTH ASH: FOR EMBERLINGS, STOVE MOODS, AND OFFICIAL APOLOGIES.
“Official apologies,” Liora said.
The stove clanked once.
“I will remember that.”
She sprinkled the hearth ash into the water.
The basin gave a low, steady glow.
The toad peeped.
Its skin pulsed brighter.
Liora reached for the Hearthglow in the only way she knew, which was not reaching at all. She thought of the moonwell. Of Cress drinking. Of the tiny sigh that had changed the whole room.
The key warmed in her pocket.
Green-gold light curled around her fingers.
Not flame.
Not smoke.
A thread of warmth with purpose in it.
She touched the basin rim.
[HEARTHGLOW USE CONFIRMED]
Action: Temporary Cooling Ring
Cost: Hearthglow 2
Effect: Stabilizes heat-safe contact zone for juvenile ember-class creature.
Duration: 3 hours
Warning: Does not prevent poor decisions.

“Then it has met us.”
The basin rim shimmered.
The toad settled when she lowered it inside, belly-first, feet splayed, eyes wide.
For one terrible second, it did nothing.
Then it sank into the warm ash-water with a sound of complete satisfaction.
Plop.
Its glow steadied.
The smoking stopped.
Liora sagged against the kitchen table.
Cress lowered himself beside the basin with the careful bend of too-long legs. His nose hovered above the water.
The toad drifted toward him.
Cress sniffed.
The toad licked his nose.
Cress sneezed silver dust into the basin.
The basin sparkled.
The toad opened its mouth and ate a sparkle.
“Apparently that is happening,” Liora said.
The system chimed.
[THERMAL STABILIZATION PARTIAL]
Resident: Ember-Toad
Overheated reduced to Warm
Unregulated Sparking reduced to Intermittent
Fire Risk: Moderate → Low with supervision
Bond Trust: 0% → 5%
Immediate Need Remaining: appropriate starter meal
Recommended Meal:
- Hearth ash
- Soft oats
- Warm mineral water
- Pinch of charcoal
- No receipts

The toad looked offended at the last line.
“So you can read.”
It blinked.
“Convenient.”
Liora found oats.
She found charcoal.
She found mineral water in a stone bottle under the sink, wedged behind a jar of preserved something that waved at her through the glass.
She left the waving jar alone.
There were relationships one did not begin before dawn.
The meal looked terrible.
It was grey, lumpy, faintly smoking, and smelled like porridge that had made unfortunate life choices.
The toad loved it.
It attacked the bowl with its whole face.
Cress watched with solemn interest.
“You cannot have any,” Liora told him.
Cress lowered his ears.
“No. That is ash.”
Lower.
“You have mineral milk.”
Lower still.
“You are being emotionally inefficient.”
Cress made a soft, wounded sound.
Liora sighed.
“I will warm your milk.”
Cress perked up.
The toad paused mid-mouthful, grey mush on its nose, and peeped.
“And you have already eaten part of the filing system.”
The toad resumed breakfast.
Liora warmed Cress’s mineral milk on the stove. The stove behaved beautifully after she told it that its restraint was noted for future commendation.
This was probably a mistake.
Stoves should not have ambition.
Still, the milk warmed.
Cress drank in small, careful sips from a chipped blue bowl, and the toad ate ash porridge in the washbasin, and for seven whole breaths nothing caught fire.
Seven breaths was not a long time.
It felt historic.
Liora leaned back against the cupboard and let her eyes close.
Her skirt was damp.
Her sleeve had two smoking holes.
Her hands smelled of ash, moon salt, old iron, and tea.
Her back had entered formal protest.
The system chimed.
Liora opened one eye.
“Make it kind.”
[CARE EVENT COMPLETE]
Event: First Hatchling Stabilization
Resident: Ember-Toad
Outcome: Stable under supervision
Bond Trust: 5% → 8%
Status: Warm | Hungry | Highly Supervised
Risk: Low with cooling ring active
Nursery Comfort: 1 → 2
Resident Count: 1 → 2
New Log Entry Available:
Ember-Toad, juvenile
Hearthglow Remaining: 2
Custodian Note:
Heat is not harm when given somewhere safe to go.

Liora read the last line twice.
Heat is not harm.
She looked at the toad, who had fallen asleep face-first in its bowl.
Grey mush bubbled gently around its nose.
Somewhere behind her, the stove gave a tiny approving tick.
Cress finished his milk and took two delicate steps toward the basin.
“Careful,” Liora said.
Cress ignored her in the gentle way creatures ignored warnings when comfort was obvious.
He folded himself down beside the basin.
The toad stirred.
One orange eye opened.
For a moment, Liora prepared to intervene. She imagined sparks. She imagined panic. She imagined explaining to an inspector that yes, the nursery’s first resident had been singed by its second resident during an unscheduled cuddle.
The form would be awful.
Cress lowered his head.
The toad wriggled forward until one warm foot rested against Cress’s folded leg.
Cress’s horn glowed softly.
The toad’s belly glowed back.
Moonlight and emberlight met on the kitchen tiles.
Not bright.
Not dramatic.
Enough.
Liora stayed very still.
Her throat did the inconvenient thing again.
“No,” she told it under her breath. “We are not crying in the kitchen. The kitchen has seen enough moisture.”
The Ledger, which had somehow dragged itself to the kitchen doorway, thumped once.
A page fluttered.
RESIDENT NICKNAME AVAILABLE
Liora looked at the basin.
The toad had curled into a little orange comma against Cress.
It smoked faintly.
It looked like a coal that had decided, after serious consideration, to become someone’s problem.
“Cinder,” she said.
The toad did not move.
Then one back foot twitched.
The system gave a soft chime.
[RESIDENT NICKNAME REGISTERED]
Ember-Toad: Cinder
Bond Trust: 8% → 9%
Warning:
Nickname affection may increase future furniture loss.
Proceed?

“Too late,” Liora said.
Cinder burped in his sleep.
A tiny smoke ring rose from his mouth, drifted through Cress’s hornlight, and became a silver-edged halo over the washbasin.
Cress watched it until it vanished.
Then he yawned.
Cinder yawned too, as if the idea had been professionally recommended.
Liora sat on the kitchen floor because standing had become unnecessary paperwork.
The stove hummed low.
The basin glowed.
The nursery creaked around them, old boards settling, pipes ticking, walls remembering they had not been empty forever.
The warmth did not fix the cracked glass.
It did not pay the fees.
It did not repair the roof, summon staff, clean the habitats, silence the unknown noises in the walls, or explain how Liora was supposed to pass inspection in six days with one mooncalf, one ember-toad, two Hearthglow, and no clean towels.
The Ledger wrote beside her.
Current Threats: deferred maintenance, unpaid fees, limited supplies, thermal supervision, laundry
Liora stared at the last word.
“Laundry is not a threat.”
The Ledger underlined it.
Twice.
A slow smile pulled at her mouth.
Cress slept with his nose near Cinder’s basin.
Cinder slept with one warm foot against Cress’s leg.
The kitchen smelled of ash porridge, warm milk, old herbs, and the first fragile suggestion that a condemned building might remember how to be useful.
Liora rested her head against the cupboard.
“Well,” she whispered. “That’s two.”
The stove clicked.
The Ledger thumped.
From somewhere deep in the nursery wall, something answered.
A soft knock.
Then silence.
Liora opened her eyes.
Cress did not wake.
Cinder snored a single puff of smoke.
The Ledger turned one page.
Slowly.
RECOMMENDED NEXT ACTION: INVENTORY OF RUIN
Liora looked at the sleeping creatures.
Then at the smoking towel.
Then at the cracked bowl, the ash on the floor, the offended teapot, the spider taking notes from a saucer, and the nursery that had apparently decided to begin making suggestions.
“Fine,” she said.
The wall knocked once more.
Liora sighed.
“But first, I am finding another towel.”
