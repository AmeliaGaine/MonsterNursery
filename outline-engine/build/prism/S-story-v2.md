## Did the "Pike has no face" gripe get fixed?  **YES.**

This was my exact prior line: *"Pike has no face yet… the living antagonist gets none of
[Edda's characterization]… a credible threat-shape, not yet a mind I've watched operate."*

The recast answers it as if written to the note. Crane IS Pike's face on the ground (CANON
ROMANCE REVISION, verbatim: "Crane is also Pike's human face on the ground (fixes the
faceless-antagonist note)"). And it's not a label — it's *dramatized* across the back half:
the believer who, like Liora in Ep1, finally won't be the apparatus's hand. The Ep1-mirror
("refuses the cull") is the move that converts a force-I'm-told-about into a person-I-watch-
choose. Gripe closed.

## The NEW-concern check (the one I went in worried about)

**Q: Does Crane-defection-enabling-the-climax weaken the seeded charter-supersession win?
Does it REPLACE the planted A2 doctrine?**  → **No. Verified clean.**

This was the trap, and the outline saw it coming and disarmed it explicitly. Ep45 states the
design intent in its own header and pays it on the page:

- The seizure still breaks because the **1842 charter outranks the order** — the A2
  supersession doctrine seeded Ep6/Ep8/Ep17, used three small wins before it pays the climax.
  The charter is Liora's, in the drawer; she files it; Quill countersigns it.
- Crane "**does not hand her the charter… He hands her the *clear ground to file it on*** — by
  refusing to be the hand that certifies the order past it." His refusal removes the
  *executor's certification* the order needed to press past a contest. The winning instrument
  is unchanged.
- Ep45 names it twice, flatly: "**His defection ENABLES the supersession climax; it does NOT
  replace it.**" And in Lands-here: "the supersession mechanism is untouched (the charter
  still wins), Crane's refusal only removes the executor's certification the order needed."

So the seeded payoff (the thing that earned half my prior score) survives intact. Crane is a
*lever that clears the ground*, not a *deus ex that wins the case.* The win is still care +
cleverness + the planted charter, taken at the bottom of the Hearthglow jar (~3). That's the
correct way to add a romance-climax beat to an already-load-bearing finale: additive, not
substitutive. My worry was specifically that the defector would become the win and retire the
charter to set-dressing — he doesn't.

**One residual flag (minor, not a deduction):** the Ep45 turn now carries a LOT — charter
supersession + Crane's defection + Liora's out-loud claim + the number-up + the empty jar, all
on essentially one beat. The outline sequences them and caps interiority (M3 trim), so it
*should* read as a chord rather than a pile-up — but it's the single most freighted beat in
the book, and on the page it lives or dies on the prose keeping four payoffs from stepping on
each other. A confidence note, not a hole.

## Second new concern (smaller)

**Romance-as-tension vs. the cozy promise.** Crane "can shut her down is the man she's falling
for" is a genuinely good stakes-raiser, and it's capped correctly (no kiss/declaration, "almost"
at Ep48). My only watch-item: the rivals-to-lovers banter is now the Arc-3 texture engine, and
if the prose lets the will-they-won't-they crowd the grief-mystery (Edda/Rhea), the book trades
its best thing (the ghost) for its most commercial thing (the slow-burn). The outline braids
them (Crane finds Rhea's erasure; his crack mirrors Liora's), which is the right instinct — but
it's a balance the prose has to hold. Scored generously, noting the tightrope.

## The ONE thing still holding me back

**Pike-himself is still off-page — the face is his *proxy's*, not his.** Crane fixes
"faceless," and that's worth the +0.7. But the architect of the fear-economy still never gets a
scene where *I* watch *his* mind operate; I infer Pike through Crane and through one filed form
(Ep49). That's a real upgrade from "named weight," but it's one remove: I've now watched the
hand, not yet the head. For a perfect score I'd want one Pike beat on the page — a moment where
the man who erased Rhea reasons out loud — so the Book-2 antagonist is a person I've met, not a
person I've been ably represented to. That last remove is the 0.8 I'm holding.
