# PRISM · M — Priya, the Voice / Humor reader

*"I read for a narrator I'd follow anywhere. Show me the wit that protects the warmth, the
deadpan that lands, the gag that builds. I know the jokes only truly fire in prose — so I'm
rating the comic ENGINEERING and the voice PROMISE. If it reads competent-dry instead of
laugh-out-loud, I'll say so."*

---

## OVERALL: **8.5 / 10**

A genuinely encouraging outline for a voice reader. Most LitRPG outlines hand me a stat
ladder and a vague promise of "humor." This one hands me a *voice fingerprint* with five
named registers, a System that talks like Edda's grief flattened into customer-service
deadpan, and — the thing that made me sit up — **three running gags with actual escalation
charts and scheduled payoffs that detonate at the climax.** That is comic engineering, not
comic hand-waving. The half-point of doubt is whether all this fine machinery produces
laughs or merely *competent dryness*, which is the failure mode this exact aesthetic risks.

---

## SUB-SCORES

### Voice distinctiveness — **9 / 10**
CANON §"Voice fingerprint" is the best thing in the binder. Five concrete modes — dry
deflection morally immovable underneath ("No." landed softly; everyone heard it),
bureaucracy-as-metaphor ("urgency wearing noisy shoes"), the System's deadpan
customer-service-with-editorial-notes ("Current Towel: legally insufficient"; "Disagree."),
staccato one-beat timing ("Knock. / Knock. / Crack."), and tenderness-stays-in-the-dry-
register. Crucially it also names the *forbidden* mode — "the two C1 lyrical lines are the
only canon drift; do not reproduce that mode" — and the Atmosphere Overlay's voice guard
repeats it ("the fire had opinions about the draught," NOT "rain wept silver"). A voice
defined by what it refuses is a voice that *could not be anyone's*. This is Murderbot-grade
register discipline. The masterstroke is making the System's deadpan *diegetically* a dead
woman's care running unattended — the funniest voice in the book is also the saddest, which
is the Pratchett move exactly. Not a 10 only because a fingerprint is a promise; the landing
is prose's job.

### Landed-humor / wit — **8 / 10**
The comic premise is sound: a snarky bureaucratic System, a three-headed civil war, a
biscuit-thieving tentacle, and Bertram's one-syllable "Mm." as a universal verdict. These
are *characterful* jokes — they come from temperament, not from quirk, which is why I trust
them. FRICTION-SCHEDULE gives every one of 49 episodes a concrete staged beat ("never 'they
interact'"), and the synergy map's elemental axis (fire/water, light/light-allergic,
stone-calm/anxiety) means the friction is *legible* — I understand instantly why two
creatures clash, which is the precondition for the joke landing. My reservation: an outline
can promise the setup but not the *snap*. "A tentacle steals the biscuit; the food-head is
outraged" is a great engine, but whether it's funny depends on the exact sentence, the
comma, the editorial note the System appends. The outline is doing everything an outline
*can* to load the gun. Held at 8 because comic timing is unprovable on a spreadsheet.

### Running-gag engineering — **9.5 / 10**
This is where the binder earns its keep and where most cozy outlines faceplant. Three named
gags, each with an *escalation grammar* and a *payoff episode*:
- **The Biscuit Tribunal** (Kraken × Corehound): theft → formal grievance → standing
  "tribunal the house humors" → closes *warm* at Ep48 (food-head lets the theft go).
- **Bertram's puddle "Mm."** (Slime×Gargoyle → Gargoyle solo): a verdict-syllable that
  *survives the departure of its straight-man* and pays off as the book's quietest and
  deepest joke at Ep46 — "Mm." decoded as Bertram having kept Edda's post for a generation.
- **The Three-Way Vote** (Corehound-internal): deadlock-on-everything → resolves into a
  single true alarm at the Ep44 climax ("they agreed only because it's serious").

The Arc-4 durability note is the detail that won me: the fosters carry the heaviest physical
friction and *depart* across Arcs 1–3, so the three running gags are deliberately assigned
to PERMANENT CORE creatures and seeded early (Ep6/8/9/22/23) "so they are fully warmed up
before Arc 4." Someone thought about the *humor cliff* — the exact thing that kills a comic
series in its back third — and engineered against it. And all three gags pay their long arc
*at the climax*, so "the humor floor is held by payoff, not padding." That is structurally
the Pratchett running-gag-that-turns-out-to-be-the-emotional-key maneuver, and it's drawn on
a chart. I have never given this sub-score this high off an outline.

### Warmth-protecting humor (never undercuts) — **8.5 / 10**
The whole design thesis — "wit that protects the warmth" — is baked in. CANON §5 of the
voice fingerprint, "tenderness stays IN the dry register, never lyrical," is precisely the
discipline that stops a cozy book from going saccharine *and* stops the jokes from going
cruel. Grief/climax episodes (34, 36, 37, 45, 46, 47) are scheduled to carry "a single DRY
beat each, lifting humor to ~4 without breaking tone" — the book knows when to *lower* the
comedy without switching it off, which is the hard part. Bertram's "Mm." migrating from
gag to the warmest beat in the book is the warmth-protection thesis made literal. Slight
reservation: "a single dry beat" in a grief episode is a tightrope — done wrong it reads as
the narrator deflecting from feeling, which *undercuts*. The outline intends the opposite
(deadpan as a vessel for tenderness), and I believe the intent, but Ep36's "the food-head
chooses this exact moment to lodge a biscuit grievance" while documents turn the room cold
is the one place I'd watch the tonal seam in prose.

### Variety (no one-note / whimsy-fatigue) — **8 / 10**
My DNF trigger is whimsy fatigue — everything is a bit, nothing is allowed to just *be*. The
outline guards against it better than I expected. Three *distinct* gag-engines (theft-
comedy, verdict-comedy, deadlock-comedy) plus the System's editorial snark plus the synergy/
anti-synergy roster gives real tonal rotation. Better: the **dedicated low-plot ASMR/sensory
beats (Ep7, 35, 40)** explicitly turn the comedy *off* for stretches of pure texture — the
book is willing to not be funny, which is the surest cure for whimsy fatigue. The cozy floor
isn't wall-to-wall quips; it alternates warm → soft-hook → warm → hard-hook. My one worry:
the running gags are *very* tightly scheduled (the same biscuit tribunal hits 9/23/32/38/43/
44/45/48), and over-scheduling is its own risk — a gag that appears on a metronome can start
to feel dutiful rather than spontaneous. The chart that protects against the humor cliff
could, if rendered mechanically, read as the gag clocking in for its shift. Watch the
density in Arc 4.

## THE ONE THING THAT MOST RAISES MY SCORE
The running-gag engineering — three named gags, each with an escalation grammar, each
assigned to a *permanent* creature precisely to survive the fosters' departure, each paying
its long arc *at the climax* (the vote resolving to one true warning, "Mm." decoded, the
tribunal closing warm). The Arc-4 durability note proves someone saw the humor cliff coming
and built a bridge. That is the single most sophisticated comic-structure thinking I've ever
read at the outline stage.

## THE ONE THING THAT MOST LOWERS MY SCORE
The unprovable gap between *competent-dry* and *laugh-out-loud*. Every joke here is
engineered to within an inch of its life, and engineered comedy can land as merely tidy —
the setups are loaded but the *snap* lives entirely in prose I can't see. Combined with the
risk that a metronomically-scheduled gag (biscuit tribunal seven times in Arc-4-adjacent
space) reads dutiful, my worry is a book that is *cleverly built* without being *actually
funny*. The fingerprint says it knows the difference; the prose has to prove it.
