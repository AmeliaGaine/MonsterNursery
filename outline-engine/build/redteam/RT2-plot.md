# RT2-plot.md — Hostile Plot-Logic & Mystery Audit (Book 1, 49 eps)

*Auditor: RT2. Brief: find the holes in the Edda grief-engine, Pike antagonist logic,
the Ep45 paperwork-climax, conveniences/deus-ex, motivation gaps, stakes legibility,
and cross-49 contradictions. Tags: [KILL] = breaks the spine if not fixed · [MAJOR] =
serious credibility wound, fixable in revision · [MINOR] = nit / polish. Citations are
to episode beats (Ep##), ARCHITECTURE.md (§), MACRO-DOORS.md, OUTLINE.draft.md, CANON.md,
and scene files (ep-##.md).*

---

## EXECUTIVE SUMMARY

The mystery's *emotional* architecture (falling for Edda; "Mm." as a kept post) is
genuinely strong and well-seeded. The *logical* architecture has three load-bearing
cracks that the outline papers over with confident assertion rather than mechanism:
(1) the "filed the right grief" clearance condition is a metaphor doing a machine's job
and does not survive being asked *how*; (2) the Ep45 "complete its own form" climax is
asserted-clever, not set-up-clever — the winning move is invented in the climax; and
(3) Pike's entire threat model is incoherent across the Rhea-erasure precedent and the
Book-2 custodian-transfer pivot. Counts below.

**Tally: 17 findings — 3 [KILL] · 8 [MAJOR] · 6 [MINOR].**

## 1. MYSTERY LOGIC — the Edda grief-engine

### [KILL] 1.1 — "Filed the right grief" is a metaphor standing in for a mechanism. The clearance condition cannot actually fire.
*Loc: ARCHITECTURE §2A (locked answer); Ep1 ep-01.md S3; Ep47 ep-47.md S2–S3; OUTLINE (a).*

The spine's Chamber-3 answer is: the System woke for Liora because she "filed the right
grief — she quit culling; it recognizes a defector." Ep47 makes this literal: the
heir-clause names "the custodian who stops [culling]," and "Liora committed the deed on
day one (charter invoked to preserve, not cull — the Mooncalf, Ep1)."

The problem is the actual sequence in Ep1 (ep-01.md S2–S3). The System is **dormant**
until Liora *presses the green sanctuary stamp* — i.e., the System activates **as a
result of** her invoking the Briarwick charter, and then greets her "as if expecting
her." So the recognition cannot be *triggered by* the act of stopping, because the
machine is off until she has already operated its own intake mechanism. What actually
"recognizes" her is **possession of the green key + invocation of the charter** — a
credential, not a moral state. The outline wants the romantic reading (the will waited
for a defector and *detected* one) but the rendered mechanics give the mundane reading
(anyone who got the key and pressed the stamp wakes it). These are different stories and
the outline never reconciles them.

Worse: a dormant counter-bureaucracy "running unattended on a timer" (ARCHITECTURE §2A)
has **no sensor for whether the holder quit culling.** How does a preserved document
know Liora's employment history or her interior refusal? Ep47 says the recipient field
is "a deed, not a name" — but a deed has to be *observed* to be matched, and nothing in
the system observes Liora's biography. The reveal asserts detection; the machinery
provides none. As written, "she filed the right grief" is poetry, and the *literal*
clearance condition is "she had the key and pressed the button," which deflates the
entire Chamber-3 / `L-HEIR` payoff (Ep47) to a tautology: the heir is whoever inherits
the key. **This is the single most dangerous finding** because `L-HEIR` ("why her
specifically?") is one of the two depth-2 spine loops and its answer is the book's
emotional climax (Ep47). If "why her" reduces to "she happened to be handed the key,"
the parasocial pull (ARCHITECTURE §2E.1: "the System chooses Liora because she filed the
right grief") loses its earned core and becomes a told-twist.

**Fix direction:** give the clearance condition a *rendered* sensor. Either (a) the
System wakes partially for any key-holder but the *heir-recognition* (the warm greeting,
the personal Notes) fires only after an observable care-act it can measure — e.g., the
first Hearthglow earned by reduced-distress rather than by force, which the System *can*
detect because Hearthglow is its own currency; or (b) make Ep1's charter-invocation
itself the legible "stop" — Liora is mid-cull, holding the stamp, and chooses the green
over the red *inside the System's own form*, so the refusal is a logged event the
document can read. Option (b) is nearly there in Ep1 but is never named as the clearance
mechanism in Ep47; Ep47 instead leans on the un-sensable "she quit culling" biography.

### [MAJOR] 1.2 — "The System IS Edda's voice, flattened by years" survives emotionally but contradicts the warming-tachometer it's built on.
*Loc: ARCHITECTURE §2A, §2E.3 (the tachometer); Ep11, Ep23, Ep46 ep-46.md S2.*

The book's page-turn metronome is "the System's voice drifts warmer as Bond/Hearthglow
rise" (§2E.3), and the reveal (Ep46) explains this as "you getting close enough to hear
the feeling that was always written in… not the System learning to feel." But the
warming is explicitly *coupled to Liora's stats* (it warms when the Kraken's tea is
brewed right, Ep11; when bonds rise, Ep23) — i.e., it responds to *present-day events
Edda could not have written*. A static preserved document cannot warm *in response to*
the Kraken's specific tea on a specific night. So either the voice is dynamically
responsive (contradicting "preserved, flattened, dead") or it is static (contradicting
the on-page warming that responds to Liora's actions). The outline wants both. Ep46's
"cleaning the heads of a recording" metaphor only covers a *monotonic* un-flattening as
the terminal is physically tended — it does **not** explain warmth that tracks the tea
quality or the bond percentages. **Fix:** decide whether Edda left a *responsive*
standard (a program that reacts to care-metrics — defensible, and arguably more
poignant) or a *recording* (static). The current text equivocates and a careful reader
will catch the contradiction at Ep11 vs Ep46.

### [MAJOR] 1.3 — Two-thread split (Edda built it / Rhea last ran it) creates a custodianship gap nobody accounts for.
*Loc: ARCHITECTURE §3 resolutions 2–3; Ep34 ep-34.md; Ep47 ep-47.md S3.*

Edda *authored* the System "dying as the cull-economy closed in." Rhea was the *last
human custodian* before Liora, and was erased by Pike's office. Ep47's screenshot shows
Rhea as Edda's **first-named heir, struck through**. But this implies Rhea held the post
*after* Edda wrote the will — so the System already recognized and served Rhea as heir.
Then Rhea was erased. **Then the System sat dormant** until Liora. Why did the System go
*dormant* when its named heir (Rhea) was forcibly removed rather than dying naturally?
A will whose heir is violently deleted should arguably be *more* active (defending, or
seeking the next heir), not less. The outline never explains the dormancy trigger: did
the System power down when Rhea was erased, or when Briarwick was condemned, or on
Edda's death? Ep30 says the source was "running unattended, warm because it was never
let go cold" — so it was *never fully off*, which contradicts Ep1's "dormant… activates
only at the ten" (ep-01.md S3 function note). **The dormancy is asserted but its cause
and timing are undefined, and the "running warm the whole time" and "dormant until
Liora" statements directly conflict.** This is the seam between the two ghost-threads
and it is load-bearing for both `L-EDDA` and `L-HEIR`.

### [MINOR] 1.4 — The recipient clause "the custodian who stops" would have matched Rhea too — so why was Rhea "first-named" by name?
*Loc: Ep47 ep-47.md S3 (struck Rhea line under the deed-clause).*

Ep47 says Edda named the heir by *deed* ("the one who stops culling") "because she did
not know who it would be." But the same screenshot shows a *prior, struck-through named
heir: Rhea.* If Edda named Rhea by name, she *did* know who it would be, contradicting
"named a deed because she couldn't know the name." Minor because resolvable (Rhea was
named first, then Edda generalized to a deed when Rhea was lost) — but the outline
presents both as simultaneous in one screenshot and never sequences them.

### [MINOR] 1.5 — "Mm." as a generation-long kept post is lovely but raises a logistics question the book invites and dodges.
*Loc: Ep46 ep-46.md S3; Ep30 ep-30.md S3.*

Bertram has guarded the terminal "for a generation." Fine as poetry. But the outline
also makes Bertram's sag-when-he-sulks mechanic (Ep8) the *entire* Ep44 climax — the
corner has sagged "on and off for thirty-six weeks every time he blinked off post."
If Bertram has reliably kept the East-Wing terminal post for a *generation*, the
"unreliable brick who keeps wandering off" characterization (Ep8, Ep22, Ep44) is in
tension with "the most patient, constant vigil in the house." The book wants him both
flighty (for the Ep44 stakes) and eternally constant (for the Ep46 reveal). [MINOR]
because the two posts are nominally different (daylight perch vs East-Wing keystone),
but Ep44's whole tension is that *this* corner is the one he abandons — and Ep30/46 say
*this* corner is the one he never leaves. Pick one.

## 2. ANTAGONIST LOGIC — Pike & the fear-economy

### [KILL] 2.1 — Pike's Book-1 threat model and his Book-2 pivot are mutually contradictory, and the contradiction is the hinge of the whole series door.
*Loc: ARCHITECTURE §3 resolution 4, §2E.2; MACRO-DOORS L-SERIES; Ep49 ep-49.md S5.*

Throughout Book 1 the apparatus's goal is to **seize / shut down / condemn** Briarwick
(the seizure order, Ep38–45). The Book-2 pivot (Ep49) is that Pike instead wants to
**become custodian** — "a System that obeys a corpse can be made to obey me" (§3.4).
These are opposite objectives, and the outline's own logic makes the Book-1 objective
*irrational* if the Book-2 objective is Pike's real want.

Here is the contradiction the text states but does not feel: Ep47/Ep49 establish that
Pike **signed Rhea's erasure** and **"knows the System obeys a dead woman's will."**
So Pike has known for a *generation* that this System is an inheritable instrument the
fear-economy would want. Yet his office spent Book 1 trying to **destroy** it
(seizure, condemnation, dissolving the wards — Ep44 clause 9 literally orders the wards
*dissolved*). Why would the man who wants to *inherit* the will spend the whole book
trying to *raze the thing that runs it*? Destroying Briarwick destroys the terminal,
the System, the custodianship — the exact asset Ep49 says he covets. The seizure and the
custodian-transfer are incompatible: you don't bulldoze the house you're filing to
inherit.

The outline tries to cover this at Ep49 ("the apparatus cannot cull a recognized
nursery, so it files to *take* it") — but that only explains the **switch** to transfer
*after* recognition closes the seizure path. It does **not** explain why Pike, knowing
since Rhea's day that the System is inheritable and valuable, ever pursued destruction
first. If he wanted it dead, he'd have let the local clerks finish it quietly years ago
(he erased Rhea — he clearly *can*). If he wanted it inherited, he'd never have signed a
seizure that dissolves its wards. **The antagonist has no coherent want across the
book.** This is the central villain-logic hole and it sits directly under `L-SERIES`,
the one door the whole series hangs on.

**Fix direction:** Pike's Book-1 want must be *consistent* with the Book-2 want. The
cleanest version: Pike's office did **not** know Briarwick had re-woken until it became
visible (the audit at Ep24). The seizure is the *reflexive local instrument* (Grindle's
level) firing before Pike fully clocks what's there; Pike's *personal* move (transfer,
not destruction) begins exactly when recognition proves the System is live and
inheritable. But then you must **remove** the line that Pike already "knows the System
obeys a will" / "signed Rhea's erasure with that knowledge" — or make Rhea's erasure a
*cover-up of a System he wanted buried, not inherited,* and give him a *reason his want
changed* (e.g., Book-1 recognition proved the will can survive and *pass cleanly*, which
is new information that makes inheritance newly viable). As written, the two wants just
sit next to each other and contradict.

### [MAJOR] 2.2 — Why bureaucratic seizure instead of something faster — answered structurally, but Pike's *personal* restraint is never motivated.
*Loc: CANON (fear-economy premise); ARCHITECTURE §2A; Ep37, Ep38.*

The *apparatus's* slowness is well-justified: it's a fear-economy, it can only act
through instruments (clerk→inspector→audit→Commissioner), and CANON forbids razing
(promise 3) and gore (heat 4). Good. But **Pike personally** is established as a man who
erases inconvenient people with a pen (Rhea, Ep34) and who has known about Briarwick's
re-awakening since at least Ep24 (the name the System reacts to). So why does *Pike*
wait through Arcs 2–4 — twelve-plus episodes of escalating local instruments — before
acting, when he already has the demonstrated power to simply erase the custodian (he did
it to Rhea)? The structural answer ("success summons the next instrument") explains the
*apparatus's* escalation ladder but not why the *top* of the ladder, who can erase at
will, sits on his hands while the home becomes undeniable. The book needs Pike to have a
*reason to wait* (e.g., he can't erase a custodian who is *publicly visible / Town Ally /
Regional* — erasure only works on the deniable, which is why he tried to keep Briarwick
small and failed). That reason is *almost* present in the "recognized = un-erasable"
logic of Ep49 but is never stated as Pike's Book-1 constraint. Without it, Pike reads as
inexplicably passive.

### [MAJOR] 2.3 — Grindle/Quill conversions: Quill's is asserted, not dramatized; Grindle never converts and is treated as if he might.
*Loc: CANON (Quill "persuadable"); Ep12, Ep24, Ep39 ep-39 (referenced), Ep45 ep-45.md S1/S3.*

CANON marks Quill "persuadable" and Pike "Book-2." Grindle is "Head Clerk, antagonist."
The brief asks whether Grindle/Quill's conversion is earned. Findings:

- **Quill never actually converts on-page in any scene file I can see.** She "lets slip"
  exposition (Ep12, Ep24), "wavers" (Ep39, an off-page-summarized episode), and at the
  climax (Ep45 S3) simply "countersigns" because she is "persuadable (CANON)." That is
  the outline *citing canon as the reason* rather than *earning the turn*. A persuadable
  inspector who flips the book's climax should have a rendered turn — a moment where
  Liora's care changes her — not a parenthetical "(CANON)". As written her countersign
  is a deus-required signature, not a character beat. [MAJOR]
- **Grindle is the one physically executing the seizure (Ep45 S1) and never converts** —
  fine, he's the unredeemed instrument. But note the seam confusion: Ep45 has *Grindle*
  at the door executing the seizure, while Ep12/24 built *Quill* as the inspector who
  "answers to someone who wanted this to fail." The book hands the climax villain-role to
  Grindle, who has had almost no on-page presence since Ep1, rather than to Quill, whom
  it spent four arcs developing. The developed antagonist (Quill) is sidelined to a
  countersignature; the underdeveloped one (Grindle) gets the climax doorway. [MAJOR —
  misallocated antagonist investment.]

### [MINOR] 2.4 — The fear-economy "engineered fear" reveal (Ep36) over-claims and risks incoherence with a functioning society.
*Loc: Ep36 ep-36.md S3.*

Ep36's reveal is that the fear was *deliberately engineered* — "creatures that weren't
dangerous enough to justify [the cull-economy]" so Control *manufactured* the fear to
protect contracts. This is a strong beat but tips toward conspiracy-theory totality: it
implies *no* monster is genuinely dangerous, which strains the world (the book's own
creatures petrify, scald, self-combust — Ep17 scalding near-miss, Ep26 cockatrice). The
nuance the book actually wants is "care neutralizes danger better than culling does,"
not "danger was fake." Ep36 currently says the latter ("the pebbles were always
pebbles," "the fear was a budget line"). [MINOR] because it's a register/over-reach
issue, but a hostile reader will note that a world with real petrification can't have
*fully* manufactured fear.

## 3. CLIMAX CREDIBILITY — "complete its own form, clause by clause" (Ep45)

### [KILL] 3.1 — The winning move ("complete the form and it authorizes a recognition, not a seizure") is invented in the climax and set up nowhere.
*Loc: Ep45 ep-45.md S3; check against Ep1, Ep38, Ep42, Ep44.*

The mechanism: the seizure order's *final* clause "asserts the power to seize
regardless"; Liora "completes the document… and the form, completed correctly, does not
authorize a taking — it authorizes a *recognition*." This is presented as the cleverest
strike of the book. Tested hostilely, it fails on setup and on logic:

- **Setup:** The book's running motif is "read the form for its *loophole*" (Ep1, Ep38,
  Ep44 S5). That is genuinely seeded. But "completing a seizure form truthfully *inverts*
  it into a recognition certificate" is a **different and much stronger claim** — that
  the apparatus's own seizure instrument contains, latent within it, a recognition
  outcome reachable by honest completion. Nothing earlier establishes that the forms have
  this property. The loophole-reading motif sets up "find the clause that voids the
  order," not "the order is secretly a recognition application." The decisive move is a
  *new rule introduced at the moment it's needed* — the definition of paperwork-magic /
  ass-pull. [KILL for the climax's credibility, because Ep45 is the book-promise payoff
  (`L-INSPECT` + the legitimacy half of `L0`).]
- **Logic:** A seizure order and a recognition ruling are *different instruments issued
  by different authorities for different purposes.* You cannot "complete" a seizure form
  into a recognition any more than you can complete an eviction notice into a deed. The
  outline hand-waves this with "a seizure with no surviving ground is not a seizure; it
  is an incomplete filing" — but "incomplete filing" → "therefore recognized" is a
  non-sequitur. Voiding every clause of a seizure leaves you with a *dead seizure*, not
  an *affirmative recognition at Regional Renown*. The recognition (Compliance → 100%) is
  a separate, positive administrative act that the outline smuggles in on the same beat.

**Fix direction:** separate the two wins. Ep45 can legitimately *break the seizure* by
voiding its clauses (that part is well-set-up: structure certified Ep44, town Ep42,
creatures Ep43, contract-legitimacy via Rhea's erasure Ep34/37 — all genuinely
load-bearing and clever). But the *recognition* should be a distinct, separately-earned
step (the pending ruling that lands at Ep49 already exists — let Ep45 break the seizure
and let Ep49 grant recognition, which is closer to what Ep49 actually does anyway). The
"complete the form into its opposite" flourish is the weakest link and should either be
cut or genuinely seeded (e.g., establish early that the sanctuary charter contains a
clause auto-converting a *failed* condemnation into provisional recognition — plant it
in Ep1's charter, where Liora already reads the 1842 charter clauses).

### [MAJOR] 3.2 — The "won at the bottom of the jar" Hearthglow economics undercut their own claimed stakes.
*Loc: Ep44 ep-44.md, Ep45 ep-45.md (Hearthglow 9→6→3); STATSPINE Part 3/4.*

The book is very proud that the seizure is won "not from abundance" — Hearthglow spent
to ~3. But Hearthglow is defined (CANON) as *care-currency earned by care* and Ep45 says
it's spent on "holding the house calm + powering the wards + funding the close." Two
problems: (a) the *legal* close (striking clauses, completing the form) is paperwork and
comprehension, which the book elsewhere insists is **free** (Ep46/Ep47: "comprehension
is free… you don't pay Hearthglow to understand a thing"). So *why does closing the
order cost Hearthglow at all?* The drain is attributed to "holding the house calm," but
that means the cost is *incidental* (storm management) not *intrinsic* to the win — the
"bottom of the jar" drama is bolted on, not load-bearing. (b) If the wards dissolving was
clause 9's threat (Ep44), and the wards are powered by Hearthglow, then spending
Hearthglow to *power the wards through the test* is fighting the order's premise with the
very resource the order targets — fine — but the narrative claims the *legal* victory is
what empties the jar, when by the book's own rules the legal victory is free. The
emotional beat (she's spent, exhausted, holding by a thread) is good; the **economics
under it are incoherent** with the Ep46–47 "comprehension is free" rule. [MAJOR]

### [MINOR] 3.3 — Bertram's Ep44 keystone hold and Ep37 stress-test are near-duplicate beats.
*Loc: Ep37 ep-37.md S2 (Bertram holds his post under strain, doesn't sulk off) vs Ep44 ep-44.md S2–S3 (Bertram holds the corner under load, doesn't blink off).*

Both episodes run: a structural strain hits the sag-corner → Bertram is tempted to
leave → he stays / is seated by belonging → the building holds → Hearthglow spent.
Ep37 explicitly flags "this is NOT the keystone-set, that's @44" — but the reader
experiences two very similar "Bertram doesn't abandon the corner under pressure and the
house holds" climaxes seven episodes apart. The escalation (ordinary post vs
load-test-certified keystone) is real but thin. [MINOR] — risk of the Ep44 payoff
feeling pre-spent by Ep37.

## 4. CONVENIENCES / DEUS EX MACHINA

### [MAJOR] 4.1 — "The System just tells us" is pervasive, despite a guardrail (G2) specifically against it.
*Loc: G2 (NOT-LITERARY: "the mystery advances by *delivered* answers… never withheld"); System boxes throughout, esp. Ep30, Ep31, Ep37, Ep46, Ep47.*

G2 is interpreted by the outline as "no *withheld* meaning" — but the inverse failure is
just as real: the System is an omniscient narrator that *hands* the reader the
interpretation of nearly every beat. Examples: Ep34 the System tells her the alarm-head
"is never wrong about who" and pre-explains the family-flag; Ep37 the System states the
entire thematic reveal ("she was erased for success… a nursery that works cannot be
argued with, only deleted") *for* Liora; Ep46/47 the System narrates its own nature, its
own grief, and the succession-stakes in plain text. The mystery rarely makes the *reader*
assemble anything — the System assembles it and reads it aloud in deadpan. This is the
"the System just tells us" failure the brief asks about, and it's structural, not
incidental: the voice-drift tachometer (§2E.3) *requires* the System to be the
emotional-interpretation layer. [MAJOR] — the reveals are *delivered* (satisfying G2's
letter) but *unearned as reader deduction* (violating G2's spirit). The Ep47 screenshot
is the worst case: the answer arrives as a captioned exhibit the terminal renders on cue.

### [MAJOR] 4.2 — The "planted lamp / left it for whoever came the right way" device makes the whole mystery a guided tour.
*Loc: Ep30 ep-30.md (sporeglow "planted to bloom for you"), Ep31 ep-31.md S2 (lichen "cultivated, fed, timed… planted to bloom for whoever earned the light"), Ep30/31 undated prior-tenancy notes.*

Edda allegedly, while dying, (a) wrote a counter-bureaucracy, (b) encoded care-standards,
(c) cultivated a bioluminescent lichen on a timer to grow into a door-outline and bloom
into an aurora at the exact moment a worthy successor restored the room, (d) left
hand-addressed notes ("If you are reading this, you came the right way…"), and (e)
arranged Bertram's vigil. This is a *lot* of foresight for a dying clerk, and it converts
the mystery from "Liora deduces" to "Liora follows a breadcrumb trail Edda pre-laid for
her specifically." The convenience is that *every* obstacle resolves because Edda
*designed it to resolve for the right person* — the moth points true, the lichen maps the
door, the gargoyle guards the spot, the notes explain the rules. When the dead author has
pre-solved the maze, the protagonist's cleverness is reduced to *deserving* the
pre-laid solution. [MAJOR] — it's emotionally warm but logically a series of conveniences
wearing a "she planned it" hat. (Compounds 1.1: if Edda could plant a lichen-timer keyed
to "the right person," that's the sensor 1.1 says is missing — but a lichen cannot detect
"quit culling" either; it blooms on *restoration*, i.e., on labor, which anyone could do.)

### [MINOR] 4.3 — The Slime's "dissolving residue persists as a house property" is a retcon to keep a placed creature useful.
*Loc: Ep34 ep-34.md header + S1 (Slime placed Ep20, but "a saved jar of Slime-residue" dissolves the Rhea plaque).*

The redaction-remover function (Ep34, the Rhea reveal) requires the Slime, but the Slime
was sent off at Ep20. The outline solves this with "a stoppered jar of leftover residue"
kept since Ep21. Functional, but it's a visible patch to honor both "fosters are placed,
never lost" (CANON) and "the Slime uncovers the redaction." The jar is never seeded
before Ep34 needs it. [MINOR] — plant the saved jar earlier (Ep21) on-page or it reads as
convenient.

### [MINOR] 4.4 — The town's signatures "arrive before she finishes asking" (Ep42) twice over.
*Loc: Ep42 ep-42 (referenced in Ep45 S2: petition signatures on file from @42); Ep24 petition; Ep43 town turns.*

The town rescues the legitimacy case at three separate beats (Ep24 petition, Ep42
signatures arriving unbidden, Ep43 town turns Ally) and each is framed as a warm
surprise. The repeated "the community spontaneously shows up at the exact needed moment"
is a cozy-genre convenience the book leans on heavily; by Ep42 the spontaneity is
expected, which drains the Ep45 use of it as a seizure-defense lever. [MINOR].

## 5. MOTIVATION GAPS

### [MAJOR] 5.1 — Liora's "choice to stay" (Ep38, `L-LIORA` payoff Ep48) is under-motivated against the established escape hatch.
*Loc: Ep38 (chooses to stay); Ep48 (takes "Matron" without deflecting); Ep1 (she's a burned-out clerk with a charter loophole).*

Liora's arc is Liability→Belonging (`L-LIORA`). The "stay" decision is treated as the
emotional spine, but the *stakes of leaving* are never made concrete. She has no
established life to return to (good — frees her) but also no rendered *cost* to staying
beyond exhaustion. The seizure threatens the *house*, not *her* — she could walk away and
be personally safe at almost any point. The book never forces the "why doesn't she just
leave and let it go" question, so the "she chooses to stay" payoff (Ep48) lands softer
than intended. [MAJOR] for the character spine — the belonging is asserted via accumulated
warmth rather than tested against a real exit.

### [MINOR] 5.2 — Sorrel (the human who knew Edda, Ep16) is a motivation black box.
*Loc: Ep16; CANON (Dr. Sorrel, disgraced healer, knew Edda).*

Sorrel "learned her handling from Edda" and joins at Ep16 — a human who *personally knew
the dead author of the central mystery* — yet apparently never tells Liora who Edda was,
or that the System might be hers, across thirty episodes until Liora discovers it
independently (Ep31–47). Why would someone who knew Edda withhold that the nursery's
System is Edda's life's work? Either Sorrel doesn't know about the System (then her
"knew Edda" function is wasted) or she does and her silence is unmotivated (a withhold
to protect the mystery's pacing). [MINOR] — but a hostile reader asks why the live
witness to Edda never just says "that's Edda's voice."

### [MINOR] 5.3 — Why did Edda's office colleagues / Control let a "counter-bureaucracy" persist on the books at all?
*Loc: ARCHITECTURE §2A; Ep36, Ep47.*

Edda wrote rules "illegal-by-design" into the Sanctuary protocol so the *rules
themselves* reward outlawed care. If the apparatus is thorough enough to erase Rhea and
manufacture an entire fear-economy (Ep36), why did it never *find and delete the
illegal protocol itself* in the generation it ran unattended? It painted over a *plaque*
(a name) but left the entire functioning counter-bureaucracy running. [MINOR] — the
apparatus's competence is dialed up or down per scene as the plot needs.

## 6. STAKES LEGIBILITY

### [MINOR] 6.1 — "Recognized / Regional" as the win-state is abstract; the reader is told it means "untouchable" but never shown the mechanism.
*Loc: Ep49 ep-49.md S2–S3 ("the local fear-economy's instruments no longer have purchase on a recognized regional nursery").*

The entire `L0` payoff is "Briarwick recognized as real → fear-economy can no longer
touch it." But *why* recognition confers immunity is asserted, not established: the book
never sets up, earlier, that Regional Renown / Recognized status legally bars
cull-contracts and seizures. It's stated at the moment of payoff (Ep49) as if known. For
the legitimacy clock to *bite* across 49 episodes, the reader needs to understand
*earlier* what recognition would concretely buy. [MINOR] — the win is legible
emotionally, fuzzy mechanically.

### [MINOR] 6.2 — Hearthglow stakes are legible but the "spend to floor" never risks anything irreversible.
*Loc: Ep45 (drained to 3); CANON (Hearthglow earned by care, decays under neglect).*

The reserve is spent to ~3 at the climax for drama, but nothing in the rules says what
happens at *0* — no creature is endangered by low Hearthglow (G1 forbids it), so the
"bottom of the jar" tension is, on inspection, a number going low with no rendered
failure-state behind it. The stakes *feel* high but are mechanically toothless because
the guardrails forbid the only consequence (harm) that would give the empty reserve
teeth. [MINOR].

## 7. PLOT HOLES / CONTRADICTIONS ACROSS 49 EPS

### [MAJOR] 7.1 — "Dormant until Liora" (Ep1) vs "running warm the whole time, never let go cold" (Ep30/46). Direct contradiction.
*Loc: Ep1 ep-01.md S3 ("the System is dormant by design… activates only at the ten") vs Ep30 ep-30.md S1/S3 ("the source, running, unattended, warm… never let go cold") and Ep46 (the voice ran flat for a generation, talking to an empty house).*

If the System/terminal was *running the whole time* (Ep30), it was never *dormant*
(Ep1). The book needs both: dormancy for the Ep1 "it wakes for her" beat, and
continuous-running for the Ep46 "flattened by a generation of talking to an empty house"
beat. A thing that's been talking continuously for a generation didn't "wake" for
Liora. **The reconciliation isn't in the text.** Best available patch: the *terminal/voice*
ran continuously (Ep30/46) but the *custodian-facing interface / Sanctuary System
front-end* was dormant (Ep1) and the green key reactivates the front-end — but the
outline never draws this distinction, and Ep1 explicitly calls the whole System dormant.
[MAJOR] and feeds 1.1/1.3.

### [MAJOR] 7.2 — The timeline is internally inconsistent: "four months" vs four seasons.
*Loc: Ep45 ep-45.md S2 / Ep49 ep-49.md S3 ("four months from a condemned ruin"); Atmosphere Overlay (OUTLINE p.752: late winter → spring → midsummer → high summer).*

Ep49 and Ep45 both say "four months." The seasonal overlay runs **late winter (Arc 1)
→ spring (Arc 2) → late spring/midsummer (Arc 3) → high summer (Arc 4)** — that is a
span of roughly **six to seven months** (late February to August), not four. The
"four months" line appears at the two highest-emphasis moments (the climax and the
finale). [MAJOR] — pick a duration; the headline number contradicts the rendered seasons.

### [MINOR] 7.3 — Cinder's send-off deferred to Book 2, but Cinder is a "fostered → placed" creature whose whole function is the pipeline.
*Loc: ARCHITECTURE §2D.1 L-CINDER note; Ep33 (match made, parting → Bk2).*

Cinder is listed in the *fostered/transient* tier (CANON creature 7) yet is kept as a
permanent chemistry keystone with the send-off deferred. This is flagged and "resolved"
in the outline (Slime takes the Ep20 first-send-off slot), but it means a creature
defined as *placeable* is structurally un-placed, and the in-world reason (a forge-patron
is found Ep33 but Cinder just… stays) is a craft decision wearing a plot rationale.
[MINOR] — honestly surfaced by the outline already, but it remains a soft contradiction
between Cinder's roster-category and her actual handling.

### [MINOR] 7.4 — Kettle is "placed Ep41" but is also the East-Wing proximity alarm whose shrieks the open seam "draws nightly" (Ep30).
*Loc: Ep30 ep-30.md S4 ("the open seam draws the Shriekmoth nightly… standing condition") vs Ep41 (Kettle placed/sent off).*

Ep30 establishes a *standing* nightly condition: the open East-Wing seam draws Kettle's
shrieks every night. Ep41 sends Kettle away (placed with a lighthouse-keeper). After
Ep41, the "standing condition" of Kettle-as-proximity-alarm is gone, but the outline
treats it as a permanent house property when convenient (Ep30) and removes the creature
when the send-off ache is wanted (Ep41) without addressing that the East-Wing alarm is
now unmonitored. [MINOR].

### [MINOR] 7.5 — "No creature harmed, ever" (CANON promise 2 + heat 4) quietly removes all real danger from the "dangerous creatures" thesis.
*Loc: CANON promises 2/3/4; the cockatrice (Ep26/43), the scald (Ep17).*

The book's thesis is "dangerous creatures are children who can be raised" — but the
guardrails guarantee in advance that no danger ever lands (no harm, no death, near-misses
only: Ep17 scald "caught a half-second early," Ep26 petrification "fails into pebbles").
The danger is *pre-defused by genre contract*, so the thesis is never actually tested —
the creatures were never going to hurt anyone because the rules forbid it. This is a
cozy-genre feature, not a bug, but it means the "proof that care works" (the whole `L0`
spine) is proving something the book never let be in doubt. [MINOR] — flagged because the
brief asks for stakes legibility: the central proposition is structurally un-falsifiable.

## CROSS-CUTTING NOTE — the three KILLs share one root

Findings 1.1 (the un-sensable clearance condition), 3.1 (the invented climax move), and
2.1 (Pike's contradictory want) are all instances of the same authorial habit visible
throughout the scene files: **the outline states the *meaning* it wants with great
confidence and assumes the mechanism follows.** "She filed the right grief," "completing
the form recognizes it," "a will that can be inherited can be stolen" — each is a
satisfying *sentence* standing in for an unbuilt *mechanic*. The prose-layer voice (the
System's deadpan asserting the interpretation) actively *enables* this, because the System
can simply declare any of these true and the dry register makes the declaration feel
earned. The most dangerous single finding is **1.1**, because it is the answer to "why
her?" — the book's deepest hook — and under scrutiny it dissolves into "she had the key."
