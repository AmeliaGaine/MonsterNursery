# STYLOMETRIC FINGERPRINT — Briarwick Voice (Ch. 1–2)

*Forensic idiolect spec. Operationalized rules + verbatim evidence. The
REPRODUCTION RULESET (§7) is the deliverable; §1–6 are its evidence.*

Counts below are from prose only (system-panel code blocks excluded unless noted).

---

## 1. RHYTHM SIGNATURE

**Sentence-length range & variance.** Bimodal, not even. The base layer is a
1–8 word declarative; it is periodically interrupted by one 30–40 word periodic
sentence, after which the writer immediately drops back to a fragment. Variance
is **deliberate long/short alternation**, never a run of medium sentences.

- Longest (≈38 words, subordinate-loaded legal cadence): *"states that in the
  absence of an appointed director, emergency custodianship may be granted to any
  qualified municipal employee willing to assume legal responsibility for the
  intake, stabilization, and rehabilitation of a condemned juvenile monster."*
  (Ch1)
- Shortest (1 word / 2 characters): *"Mm."* (Ch1) — and the one-word fragment
  paragraph *"Miserably."* (Ch1), *"Briarwick."* (Ch1).

**Opener distribution (rough proportions):**
- Subject-first (noun/pronoun + verb): **~70%.** *"Liora rubbed the bridge of her
  nose." / "The mooncalf made a sound." / "It sat there in red municipal ink…"*
- Conjunction/negation-led fragment ("Not…", "But…", "Then…", "Or…"): **~20%.**
  *"Not hope." / "But attention." / "Then the gate creaked open by itself."*
- Adverbial/prepositional-phrase-first (scene-transition only): **~7%.** *"Across
  the room…" / "For the first time in four years…" / "By the time Briarwick's lane
  appeared…"*
- Participial-first: **~3%, rare.** *"Pressed into the far corner…"* (does not
  appear; the writer avoids the dangling-participle opener almost entirely).

**Paragraph habit.** Overwhelmingly **single-sentence paragraphs.** In Ch1, 40+
paragraphs are one short sentence standing alone (*"Stamp." / "Pollard stopped
humming." / "She saw hinges."*). Multi-sentence paragraphs (3–6 sentences) are
reserved for set-dressing/world description (the office, the nursery exterior).
The rhythm = dense column of one-liners, occasionally widening into a descriptive
block, then narrowing again.

**The recurring cadence "tell" — negation-then-fragment triad.** Define by what
it is NOT across 2–3 fragments, then deliver the real noun as its own fragment:
- *"Not a bleat. Not a cry. / A small, broken hiccup."* (Ch1)
- *"Not violently. / Messily."* and *"Not bright. / Not dramatic. / Enough."* (Ch2)

## 2. PUNCTUATION FINGERPRINT

| Mark | Frequency | Evidence |
|---|---|---|
| Em-dash — | **Occasional** (7 in Ch1, 0 in Ch2) | *"Her hand dipped—not into red ink, but into the drawer of Grindle's manifest board."* (Ch1) — used for a single mid-sentence pivot/appositive, never paired as a bracketed aside |
| Semicolon | **Never** (0 / 0) | *(none exist)* |
| Colon | **Occasional, mechanical** | In prose, a colon only introduces a displayed reveal: *"stamped on every side with a warning in red wax:"* then the line break to **DEFECTIVE — DESTROY BEFORE DUSK**. (Inflated raw counts come from form/system labels, not prose.) |
| Parenthesis | **Never** (0 / 0) | Asides are handled by em-dash or by a fresh fragment paragraph instead |
| Ellipsis | **Never** (0 / 0) | Trailing-off is rendered as a hard stop + new fragment, never "…" |
| Exclamation | **Never in prose** (0 / 0) | Even shouted/excited beats stay end-stopped with a period |

**Comma philosophy.** Rhythmic but disciplined — commas mark list-beats and the
load-bearing periodic sentence, not breath-padding. **Oxford comma: YES,
consistent.** *"dust, leaves, and one scandalized spider" / "ink, dust, and damp
wool."*

**Signature constructions:**
1. **Fragment-for-emphasis** as its own paragraph (see §1).
2. **Em-dash single pivot**, never the paired aside.
3. **Colon → line-break → block-capital reveal** for forms, signs, system text.

## 3. LEXICON & DICTION

**Register.** Mid-formal narration deliberately collided with bureaucratic
boilerplate and childlike diminutives. The comedy lives in the seam. Latinate
officialese (*authorization, liability, custodianship, stabilization,
compliance, requisition*) is set against Anglo-Saxon plainness (*soot, hiccup,
buns, nose, towel, knock*). The writer never stays in one register for a full
paragraph.

**Lexical reach.** A **tight, repeated core** with occasional precise flourishes,
not a thesaurus parade. The flourish is usually a yoked oddity (*"argumentative
pumpkins," "scandalized spider," "emergency textile reassignment"*).

**Pet words / unconscious tics (the fingerprint the writer doesn't know about):**
- **"municipal"** — load-bearing adjective applied to everything: *"municipal
  ink," "municipal handcart," "municipal cotton," "municipal capacity,"
  "municipal departments."*
- **Diminutives "tiny / little / small"** — saturation level: *"tiny
  thundercloud," "small, broken hiccup," "tiny crescent horns," "little
  flame," "tiny orange foot."*
- **Hedging stance-adverbs**: *"apparently," "probably," "frankly," "possibly."*
  *"apparently your emergency custodian" / "Grindle had probably filed a complaint
  against laughter."*
- **"Of course"** as a deadpan resignation beat: *"'Of course,' Liora said." /
  "'Of course breakfast is involved.'"*
- **The garment metaphor for abstractions** — abstractions are dressed in
  clothing/objects: *"urgency wearing noisy shoes," "morally superior in a
  cupboard," "a kettle being forgiven."*
- **The negation-reframe aphorism**: *"Care is not soft. Care is structural." /
  "Heat is not harm when given somewhere safe to go."*

**Concrete-noun-to-abstraction ratio.** Heavily concrete (rough 4:1). Abstractions
are not left bare — they are immediately leashed to an object or action: *"That was
the sentence that did most of the killing."*

## 4. FIGURATIVE & RHETORICAL HABITS

**Metaphor/simile density: moderate, ~1 per 6–8 sentences**, and the SOURCE
DOMAINS are narrow and diagnostic:
1. **Bureaucracy / institutional procedure** (the dominant well): *"the calm
   inevitability of a policy decision made by people not standing near it" /
   "crisp as folded parchment."*
2. **Machinery + emotion fused**: *"clattered and wheezed around her like an
   elderly machine that had developed strong opinions about misery."*
3. **Domestic / kitchen / light**: *"like a warm light behind a dirty window" /
   "It sounded like a kettle being forgiven."*

Notably **absent** source domains: finance, weather-as-mood, war/combat, sport.

**Pervasive device — comic personification.** Inanimate objects are granted
bureaucratic or emotional agency, deadpan: the stove *"preened,"* the Ledger
*"snapped itself shut with the injured dignity of a book that had not consented to
breakfast,"* the towel was *"morally superior in a cupboard,"* the spider is
*"waistcoated."*

**Humor mechanism: bathos + deadpan understatement.** The fixed rule: a tender or
high-stakes beat is undercut within 1–2 lines by a mundane administrative or
absurdly literal image.
- *"beneath the exhaustion was something bright and dangerous. / Purpose,
  possibly. / Or dehydration."* (Ch1)
- *"Grindle had probably filed a complaint against laughter in 3 copies."* (Ch1)

**Recurring rhetorical devices:**
- **Rule-of-three / list-ending-on-the-absurd**: *"flour theft, soot tracking,
  inappropriate giggling" / "too many legs, too few records, or relatives in
  politics."*
- **Direct address (apostrophe)** to creatures and objects: *"'Lovely knobs,' she
  said" / "'Don't test me,' she told it."*
- **Rhetorical/literalizing comeback** in dialogue (stichomythia): each retort
  takes the official phrase literally — *"'I can count that high.' / 'That sounds
  like a scheduling issue.'"*
- **Anaphora in description**: *"It sat there… It sat there… It sat there…"* (Ch1).

## 5. VOICE & STANCE

**Narrative distance.** Close third, limited to Liora, but **observational-wry
rather than confessional** — interiority is delivered through her judgments on the
external world, not through stated feeling. *"Liora found that offensive."*

**Relationship to reader.** Confides via shared irony; **never lectures, never
winks overtly.** The reader is trusted to catch the undercut. The narrator
withholds sentiment and lets the system-panel aphorisms carry the thesis (*"Care
is structural"*) so the prose never has to editorialize.

**Emotional default: contained competence over a low ache.** Feeling is **implied
through action and bodily detail, then deflected**, almost never stated:
- *"Liora's throat closed." / "Her throat did the inconvenient thing again."* —
  emotion = a physical event she manages, not a declared mood.
- *"'No,' she told it under her breath. 'We are not crying in the kitchen.'"* —
  feeling is acknowledged only to be administratively refused.

## 6. NEGATIVE SPACE (what this writer never does)

1. **Never uses a semicolon or parenthesis** — zero across both chapters.
2. **Never uses ellipses or exclamation points in narration** — trailing-off and
   excitement are both rendered as period + fragment.
3. **Never states the protagonist's emotion as an abstract label** ("she felt
   sad/happy/afraid"). Emotion is always a body part, an action, or a joke.
4. **Never lets a tender beat stand un-undercut** — sincerity is always followed
   within two lines by bathos or admin language.
5. **Never opens consecutive sentences with the same long subordinate structure**
   — no stacked "Although…/Because…/While…" cascades; subordination is rationed.
6. **Never draws metaphors from finance, weather-mood, combat, or sport.**
7. **Never uses an adverb-heavy speech tag** ("she said angrily/sadly"); tags are
   bare ("she said") and the surrounding fragment does the emotional work.

## 7. REPRODUCTION RULESET (the deliverable)

A different writer can pass a blind authorship test by mechanically obeying these:

1. **Default to subject-first declaratives of 4–9 words.** Keep ~70% of openers
   subject/pronoun-first.
2. **Never write a semicolon, a parenthesis, an ellipsis, or an exclamation
   point.** Split with a period instead. Use the em-dash only once per
   paragraph at most, and only as a single mid-sentence pivot — never paired.
3. **After any sentence of 25+ words, immediately drop to a 1–4 word fragment
   paragraph.** Pair every periodic sentence with a short reset.
4. **Make at least one paragraph in four a single sentence,** and at least one
   page-section a one-word fragment ("Miserably." "Enough.").
5. **Deploy the negation-then-fragment triad roughly once per scene:** "Not X.
   Not Y. [The real noun]."
6. **Undercut every sincere or high-stakes beat within two lines** with mundane
   administrative language or an absurdly literal image (bathos is mandatory, not
   optional).
7. **Personify the furniture, paperwork, and weather deadpan** — give objects
   bureaucratic dignity or petty emotions ("the Ledger… had not consented to
   breakfast").
8. **Pull every metaphor from bureaucracy, fused machinery-with-emotion, or the
   domestic kitchen. Never from finance, weather-mood, combat, or sport.**
9. **Seed the word "municipal" (or an institutional synonym) and a diminutive
   ("tiny/little/small") on most pages.** Sprinkle hedging adverbs: "apparently,
   probably, frankly, possibly."
10. **End lists on the absurd item.** Three or four parallel nouns; the last
    breaks the register ("…too many legs, too few records, or relatives in
    politics").
11. **Use the Oxford comma every time.** Commas mark list-beats and the one
    periodic sentence only — no breath-padding commas.
12. **Render emotion as a body event the character then refuses** ("her throat
    closed," "we are not crying in the kitchen"). Never label the feeling
    abstractly.
13. **Keep speech tags bare ("she said"); never modify them with an adverb.** Let
    the adjacent fragment carry tone.
14. **Build dialogue as fast single-sentence stichomythia where each retort takes
    the official's phrase literally** ("It requires personal liability." / "I
    assumed.").
15. **Have the character address objects and animals directly** ("Lovely knobs").
16. **Reserve abstract thesis statements for an external "system/sign" voice**
    set off in block format, phrased as negation-reframe ("X is not Y. X is Z."),
    so the narrator never editorializes.
17. **Close each chapter/section on a short line of spoken dialogue, ideally a
    running tally** ("'Well,' she whispered. 'That's two.'").
18. **Use digits (not spelled numbers) when the number is itself the joke** ("a
    complaint against laughter in 3 copies"); spell numbers otherwise.

## 8. CALIBRATION TEST (new topic: a train station)

Obeying the ruleset (subject-first base, bathetic undercut, personified
infrastructure, negation-triad, em-dash single pivot, no semicolon/parenthesis,
Oxford comma, bare close):

> The departures board flickered through its options with the weary patience of a
> clerk who had explained the same delay forty times. Not cancelled. Not on time.
> Pending—which was the station's favorite word, because pending committed to
> nothing, disappointed everyone equally, and required no apology whatsoever.
